Animating Stories: What I’ve Learned

Process, Practice, and Professional Goals

Animation, for me, is the craft of sculpting time—shaping motion until it carries meaning. This semester pushed me to think beyond polish and focus on clarity: readable silhouettes, motivated arcs, and poses that communicate before a single frame plays. I learned to plan with thumbnails, block quickly, and only then add the nuance that makes moments feel alive.

I’ve become more intentional with references. Instead of copying movement, I break it into beats—anticipation, action, overshoot—and test each beat in blocking. That mindset also made graph editor work less scary: spacing became a design choice, not a fight with tangents. The result is cleaner timing and a stronger sense of weight.

Technically, I practiced rig hygiene and small tools that save hours later: naming conventions, non-destructive constraints, and viewport lighting for quick reads. I also built a habit of daily micro-studies—ten-to-twenty second cycles that target a single idea like follow-through or eye darts. They’re fast, but they compound.

Professionally, my goal is story-driven 3D character work. Short term, I’m focusing on a tight reel: three short shots that each demonstrate a different strength—subtle acting, physicality, and multi-character interaction. Long term, I want to collaborate on projects where performance and design are inseparable, and where the animation serves the narrative above everything else.
